Article 01 · Tender Offer · Valuation · Tax Strategy

The Company Says It's a Great Valuation.
But Is It Great For You?

You're being told this is a strong price. The email says "attractive valuation," "employee liquidity opportunity," "limited window." It feels like a win. You've been waiting for this moment for years.

But here's what nobody in that email tells you: you're evaluating the headline price. You should be evaluating the after-tax outcome — and your remaining concentration risk once the dust settles.

Your Situation — Real Example

You own 20,000 shares. Tender offer price: $25/share. Total value: $500,000. You're allowed to sell 50%, so you think: "I'll take $250K off the table." Makes sense. Here's the math you're skipping:

Your basis is $5/share. The gain is $20/share. On 10,000 shares, that's $200,000 of taxable gain. Federal capital gains at 20%, plus Net Investment Income Tax at 3.8%, plus California state tax at roughly 10% — and your $250K becomes $160K–$170K after tax. And you still hold $250K in paper value tied to one company.

What Happens If You Do Nothing Strategically

You sell randomly (or not at all). You don't model the taxes. You keep most of your exposure. You overestimate your liquidity. You remain concentrated. And if the valuation drops before the next round — you've lost both the paper gains and the diversification window you missed.

If valuation drops after this window → you lose the gains AND the missed diversification. Both.

Misaligned Interests — The Real Problem

The company wants a clean tender, a validated valuation, and happy employees who participate. You want real liquidity, tax efficiency, and risk reduction. Those are not aligned objectives. Institutional investors on the other side of this deal have a full team — legal, financial, tax. You have the summary deck they prepared for you.

What To Do Instead

1
Model after-tax proceeds first — not the headline number. What actually hits your account after federal, NIIT, and state taxes? That's your real decision basis.
2
Set a target concentration limit — ask yourself: what percentage of my total net worth should remain in this single company? If the answer is under 30%, adjust your sell amount accordingly.
3
Treat it like an institutional trade — partial exit, risk-managed hold, reallocation plan ready before you sign anything.

Independent advice isn't paranoia. It's table stakes. The window is typically 20–30 days. The tax structure of how you receive proceeds can vary significantly based on grant type (IRC §83, §1221). Model it before the deadline, not after.

Article 02 · RSU · ISO · AMT · Equity Sequencing

The Type of Equity You Hold
Determines Everything.

You think: "It's all stock comp." It's not. RSUs and ISOs behave completely differently — and if you sequence them wrong, you can create a six-figure tax problem without knowing it.

Your Situation — Real Example

You have RSUs vesting this year worth $300,000. You also have 10,000 ISOs at a $2 strike, with current fair market value of $20. What most people do: they let RSUs vest (taxed automatically), then exercise ISOs in the same year because "it's time." Here's what actually happens:

The $300K in RSUs is taxed as ordinary income. Federal plus California comes to roughly 40–50%. That's $120K–$150K in tax. Then the ISO spread — $18/share on 10,000 shares — creates $180,000 of Alternative Minimum Tax income. You just triggered AMT on top of ordinary income in the same year. Total incremental tax bill: often $100,000+.

The Critical Difference

RSUs are a tax event you don't control — they vest, you owe, full stop. RSUs are taxed as ordinary income at vesting (IRS Pub 525). ISOs are a tax event you do control — you choose when and whether to exercise. ISOs can trigger AMT on the spread between strike and FMV at exercise (IRC §56–§57). The spread at exercise is AMT income even though you receive no cash.

RSU income + ISO AMT stacked in the same year = the most common six-figure mistake we see.

The Sequencing Rule

1
Accept RSU income as fixed — plan around it, not with it. Withholding often underestimates your true tax. Reconcile before year-end.
2
Run AMT projections before ISO exercise — calculate your AMT exposure in the year you plan to exercise. Use lower-income years strategically.
3
Sequence ISOs across tax years — exercise gradually, or consider early exercise (83(b) election) when the spread is still small and FMV is low.
4
Start the holding period clock deliberately — ISOs get capital gains treatment if held 1 year post-exercise AND 2 years from grant date. The clock only runs if you act.

Same company. Same total compensation. Two employees — one pays $80K in tax, one pays $200K+. The difference is not intelligence. It's sequencing. Nobody explains this because equity comp advisors and tax advisors rarely sit in the same room.

Article 03 · Pre-IPO · Lockup Strategy · Wealth Realization

You Built Wealth on Paper.
Now Don't Lose It in Execution.

You're heading toward an IPO. Your equity is worth $1M+, maybe more. And you're thinking: "I'll just hold. It's going higher." The problem isn't your conviction. The problem is that you don't control lockups, market conditions, or post-IPO volatility — and those three variables are what determine whether your paper wealth becomes real wealth.

What Actually Happens at IPO — Real Example

Equity value at IPO pricing: $1.2M. Lockup period: 180 days. IPO price: $30/share. During the lockup, the stock drops to $22. You're still locked. Your $1.2M is now $880K on paper — and you can't do anything about it. Six months of watching matters you can't control.

The lockup expiration date is your first real decision point. Plan before it arrives — not after.

The 3-Bucket Strategy

1
Liquidity Bucket — Sell enough to cover taxes on the equity income event AND fund 1–2 years of life. This is not optional. If you under-withhold, the IRS will find you. Size this bucket first.
2
Core Hold Bucket — Maintain meaningful upside exposure to the company you helped build. Set a price target or time horizon. This is intentional, not passive.
3
Diversification Bucket — Systematically redeploy into uncorrelated assets: index funds, real estate (see below), fixed income. This is how paper wealth becomes a portfolio.

The Tax Stacking Problem

IPO year is often the highest-income year of your life. RSUs vest at IPO price. Options exercise creates spread income. State bonus withholding kicks in. The combination can push your effective rate above 50% in California. Planning the sequence of what vests, when, and how it's taxed can save real money — but only if you act before the window, not after.

You're not "selling early." You're converting risk into optionality. The upside remains. The downside is limited. And you stop being fully exposed to a single outcome you can't control.

Article 04 · Merger · Equity Conversion · All-Stock Deal

You Don't Own
What You Think You Own.

Your company got acquired. Your equity converted. And you're assuming: "It's basically the same." It is not. In an all-stock acquisition, every parameter of your equity award changes simultaneously — and the merger documents describing those changes are written for lawyers, not for you.

Your Situation — Real Example

You had 5,000 options at a $5 strike. Conversion ratio in the all-stock deal: 0.5. Now you have 2,500 options at a $10 strike. Your share count is cut in half. Your strike has doubled. And the spread — which drives your AMT exposure — is completely different. Before: FMV $25, spread $20. After conversion: FMV $25, strike $10, spread $15. Your AMT calculation has changed materially, and you didn't get a memo.

Three Mistakes We See Repeatedly

1
Modeling AMT against the old strike — not the adjusted one. The AMT is calculated on the spread at exercise. If you're using pre-merger numbers, you're modeling the wrong tax liability.
2
Assuming old vesting still applies — Vesting schedules can be modified at the close of a deal, buried in the "treatment of equity awards" summary rather than your standard grant doc. Read it.
3
Waiting for the IPO filing — By the time the S-1 is public, the optimal planning window is gone. The pre-filing period is when strategic options are open. After filing, the blackout clock starts.
What to do this week: Get the conversion ratio in writing → Recalculate strike, spread, share count → Model AMT on the new numbers → Understand the blackout start date.

Rebuild From Zero

Step 1: Confirm conversion math — ratio, new strike, new share count — in writing from HR or the plan administrator. Step 2: Recalculate the spread. This drives the AMT calculation. Step 3: Re-evaluate your exercise timing and sale sequencing against the new numbers. Step 4: If there's an IPO coming, map the decision windows before the blackout, not during it.

The planning window in a post-merger pre-IPO situation is narrow. The employees who act before the S-1 filing have options. The ones who wait until it's "clear" are working with whatever remains.

Article 05 · Concentration Risk · Asset Basket · Real Estate · LA Tech Workers

Half Your Net Worth Is
One Company's Stock.

You came in doing everything right. Maxing your 401k. No consumer debt. Saving aggressively. Smart, disciplined. And yet: 60% of your total net worth is in the equity of a single company — the same company that pays your salary, your health insurance, and your bonus. That's not a portfolio. That's a concentrated bet on a single counterparty, three times over.

The Math of Concentration

Net worth: $2M. Company stock: $1.2M. Diversified assets: $800K. If the stock drops 50%, your net worth drops to $1.4M. You just lost $600K. And unlike a diversified portfolio, there's no other leg of the stool to hold you up — because your income, your equity, and your savings are all moving in the same direction at the same time.

A concentrated position isn't a reward. It's a risk that hasn't been repriced yet.

The 5-Layer Asset Basket for LA Tech Employees

1
Liquidity layer (10–20%) — 6–12 months of cash, separate from your emergency fund. This is the buffer that lets you make strategic decisions instead of reactive ones during a downturn.
2
Tax-advantaged base — 401k maxed, backdoor Roth if income limits apply, HSA if eligible. These are the closest things to guaranteed after-tax return that exist in the tax code.
3
Real assets (real estate) — Primary residence equity, West LA multifamily, or REITs. Real estate provides depreciation offset against your ordinary income, which is significant when your W-2 income is in the top bracket. (See our underwriting model below.)
4
Diversified equity (not your employer) — Index funds, sector exposure outside tech. The goal is non-correlation with your employment income. If the tech sector corrects and you lose your job, this layer should not be moving in the same direction.
5
Equity optionality (your company stock) — Intentionally sized, not defaulted to. Set a target: under 30% of net worth in a single position. Use liquidity events to systematically reduce toward that target.

The Tax Angle — Why Real Estate Belongs in This Basket

If you're making $400K–$700K in W-2 income plus RSU income in a high year, your marginal California rate plus federal is above 50%. Real estate depreciation directly offsets ordinary income — $50K in depreciation on a $1.5M property can reduce your tax bill by $25K+ per year. It's one of the few remaining tax shelters available to high earners without complex structures.

Article 06 · Behavioral Finance · Selling Psychology · Equity Realization

$800K on Paper Feels Different
Than $800K in Your Account.

You have $800K on paper. And you hesitate to sell. Here's why, exactly: selling makes it final. If you sell and the stock goes to $1.2M, you were wrong. If you hold and it goes to $300K, at least you didn't make an active mistake. The brain treats selling as a decision you can be judged for — and holding as something that just happened to you.

That asymmetry is a cognitive trap. And it's expensive.

Not Selling Is Also a Decision

Example: Stock goes from $800K to $500K during a correction. You didn't sell. You lost $300K of real opportunity — a down payment on a Venice Beach fourplex, two years of runway to start something, a diversified portfolio that would still be whole right now. The "no decision" still cost you $300K.

The freeze is the most common wealth destruction event we see. Inaction is a position.

The "Sell Enough to Make It Real" Framework

1
Define a concrete use for the proceeds — not "diversification" in the abstract. A specific down payment. A specific debt paid off. A specific investment. Make the money do something visible before you sell it.
2
Size the sale to fund that concrete thing — not everything. Not a round percentage. Just what it takes to fund the thing you identified. Keep the rest. You haven't abandoned the upside; you've carved out a floor.
3
Set a tax-aware timeline — partial sales can be sequenced across tax years, or structured using covered calls or collars if you want to limit downside without triggering a sale event in the current year.
4
Hold the rest with an explicit thesis — not inertia. If you're holding because you believe in a specific outcome at a specific time horizon, that's a position. If you're holding because selling feels irreversible, that's the freeze. Know which one it is.

The shift is from "all or nothing" to "partial realization plus upside retention." Some of it becomes real. The rest keeps working. You stop living in between — neither invested nor committed — and start making intentional decisions with real money.
